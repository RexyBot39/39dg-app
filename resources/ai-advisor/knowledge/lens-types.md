# Lens Types

## Single Vision Lenses

Single vision lenses correct vision at one distance — either near, intermediate, or far. They are the most common lens type and work well for most people who need correction for one focal point.

- **Distance lenses** are used for driving, watching TV, and general daily activities.
- **Reading lenses** are used for close-up tasks like reading books, looking at a phone, or doing detailed work.
- **Computer/intermediate lenses** are focused at a middle distance, typically around arm's length, for screen use.

Single vision lenses are compatible with nearly every frame style.

## Reading Lenses

Reading lenses are single vision lenses set to a near focal distance. They are suitable for people who need help seeing clearly up close but do not need distance correction.

Over-the-counter reading lenses use the same power in both eyes. If your eyes need different powers, a prescription pair from an eye care professional will give you a more accurate fit.

Reading lenses are not intended for driving or extended distance use.

## Progressive Lenses

Progressive lenses provide a smooth, gradual transition between distance, intermediate, and near vision in a single lens — with no visible line. They replace bifocals and trifocals for most wearers.

The top portion of the lens handles distance vision, the middle handles intermediate tasks like computer work, and the bottom handles reading and close-up tasks.

Progressive lenses require a brief adjustment period. Most people adapt within a few days to a few weeks.

## High-Index Lenses

High-index lenses are made from a denser optical material that bends light more efficiently. This allows them to correct stronger prescriptions with thinner, lighter lenses than standard plastic.

Higher index numbers indicate thinner lenses:
- **1.57 index** — entry-level thin option, works well for mild to moderate prescriptions
- **1.61 index** — noticeably thinner, suitable for moderate prescriptions
- **1.67 index** — a significant step up; good for stronger prescriptions
- **1.74 index** — among the thinnest available; ideal for very strong prescriptions

High-index lenses are especially recommended for anyone with a prescription outside the range of roughly +/-3.00 or higher cylinder values, as standard plastic lenses may become thick or heavy at those powers.

High-index lenses generally pair well with anti-reflective coating, which reduces internal reflections common in denser lens materials.

## Polycarbonate Lenses

Polycarbonate lenses are impact-resistant, lightweight, and offer built-in UV protection. They are a popular choice for:

- Children's eyewear
- Safety glasses and sport frames
- Rimless or semi-rimless frames where lens strength matters
- Everyday wearers who want added durability

Polycarbonate lenses have a refractive index of approximately 1.586, making them thinner than standard plastic (CR-39) but not as thin as higher-index materials.

They are a practical, affordable upgrade for most prescriptions in the mild to moderate range.
