# Frame Guide for Stronger Prescriptions

## Why Frame Choice Matters with Strong Prescriptions

At higher prescription powers, lens thickness becomes a practical consideration. The optical properties of stronger prescriptions create thicker lens edges (in minus prescriptions) or thicker lens centers (in plus prescriptions). Frame choice directly affects how thick and heavy your finished lenses will be.

The right frame can minimize visible edge thickness and keep lenses lighter and more comfortable. The wrong frame can make an otherwise manageable prescription look and feel heavier than it needs to.

## What "Strong Prescription" Generally Means

As a general guideline, these ranges are where frame choice becomes more important:

- **Sphere (SPH)**: above +/-3.00
- **Cylinder (CYL)**: above +/-2.00
- **Progressive ADD**: above 2.00 (combined with high sphere or cylinder)

These are approximate. The specific shape and size of the frame affect outcomes more than the prescription number alone.

## Key Frame Recommendations for Stronger Prescriptions

### 1. Choose Smaller Lens Sizes

The single most effective thing you can do for a strong prescription is choose a smaller lens. Edge thickness increases toward the outer edges of the lens — a smaller lens means less distance from center to edge, and therefore thinner edges overall.

- Target lens widths in the **46mm–52mm** range when possible
- Avoid very wide frames (56mm+ lens width)

### 2. Choose Round or Oval Shapes

Round and oval frames have the shortest distance from lens center to edge in all directions. This reduces the maximum edge thickness compared to angular or rectangular shapes with hard corners.

- Rectangular frames have longer diagonal distances to the corners, which can be the thickest point
- Round and oval shapes minimize this effect

### 3. Choose Full-Rim Frames

Full-rim frames surround the lens completely, which conceals edge thickness. Semi-rimless or rimless frames leave the lens edges exposed, which can make lens thickness more visible.

Full-rim frames also provide better structural support for high-index lenses, which are thinner and may be more vulnerable to stress in rimless mounts.

### 4. Consider High-Index Lenses

High-index lens materials make lenses thinner at the same prescription power. For stronger prescriptions, upgrading to a higher index (1.67 or 1.74) can make a meaningful difference in finished lens thickness and overall weight.

See **lens-materials.md** for high-index options.

### 5. Choose Metal or Lightweight Frame Materials

Since high-index lenses are inherently denser materials, pairing them with a lightweight frame helps balance total weight. Titanium and other lightweight metal frames are a good pairing with high-index lenses.

### 6. Avoid Very Tall Frames for Minus Prescriptions

For minus (distance) prescriptions, taller lenses mean more edge area and more visible edge thickness at the top and bottom of the lens. A moderately tall frame tends to work better than an oversized or very tall frame.

### For Progressive Wearers with Strong Prescriptions

Progressive wearers with strong prescriptions have an additional consideration — the frame must have sufficient vertical height for the progressive design (at least 28mm, preferably 30mm+), but should not be so large that it amplifies lens thickness. A medium-height full-rim frame in a round or slightly rectangular shape often strikes the best balance.

## Summary

| Goal | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Minimize edge thickness | Smaller lens width (46mm–52mm) |
| Even edge thickness | Round or oval lens shape |
| Hide edges | Full-rim frame |
| Reduce total weight | High-index lenses + lightweight frame |
| Progressive with strong Rx | Full-rim, 30mm+ vertical height, moderate width |
